Section A

Introduction & Epidemiology

Key Statistics

Global Burden

  • ~700,000 deaths/year from colorectal cancer
  • Rectal cancers: 30–40% of all colorectal cases
  • Incidence rising in adults <50 in high-income countries

Clinical Outcomes

  • Median age at diagnosis: 60–72 years
  • 5-year survival: stage-dependent
  • Stage I: ~90%
  • Stage IV: <15%
Key Point: Early detection and stage determine prognosis

Risk Factors

Non-modifiable

  • Age
  • Family history
  • Lynch syndrome (HNPCC)
  • FAP
  • Prior colorectal neoplasia

Modifiable

  • Diet: high red/processed meat
  • Obesity
  • Physical inactivity
  • Smoking
  • Heavy alcohol
  • IBD (UC > Crohn's)

Molecular

  • MSI-high (Lynch)
  • Different natural history
  • Therapeutic implications
  • Immunotherapy response
Screen high-risk patients earlier. MSI testing informs both prognosis and therapy.

Section B

Rectal Anatomy

Rectal Segments

Clinical Division

  • Rectum length: 12–15 cm from anal verge
  • Lower rectum: 0–5 cm
  • Mid rectum: 5–10 cm
  • Upper rectum: 10–15 cm
Surgical Relevance:
• Sphincter preservation likelihood
• Approach selection (LAR vs APR)

Anatomical Relations

Relations

  • Anterior:
    • Men: prostate, seminal vesicles
    • Women: vagina, uterus
  • Posterior: presacral fascia, sacrum
  • Lateral: pelvic sidewalls, neurovascular bundles

Peritoneal Coverage

  • Upper rectum: partly intraperitoneal
  • Mid/low: extraperitoneal
Peritoneal reflection affects recurrence patterns and surgical approach

Vascularization

Arterial Supply

  • Superior rectal artery
    • Terminal branch of IMA
    • Main supply to upper rectum
  • Middle rectal arteries
    • From internal iliac
    • Variable, supply mid rectum
  • Inferior rectal arteries
    • From internal pudendal
    • Supply anal canal and distal rectum

Venous Drainage

  • Superior rectal vein → IMV → portal system
    • ⚠ Liver metastasis risk
  • Middle/inferior rectal veins → internal iliac → systemic
    • ⚠ Lung metastasis pathway
Dual venous drainage explains metastatic patterns

Lymphatic Drainage

Primary Pathways

  • Pararectal nodes
  • ↓
  • Superior rectal nodes
  • ↓
  • Inferior mesenteric nodes
Lateral Pathway:
Internal iliac nodes — more relevant for low rectal tumors

Clinical Relevance

  • Nodal status determines neoadjuvant therapy
  • TME removes mesorectal nodes en bloc
  • Lateral pelvic node dissection: consider in confirmed metastases (center-dependent)
MRI assessment of lateral nodes guides surgical planning

Section C

Pathophysiology & Carcinogenesis

Carcinogenesis Pathways

Chromosomal Instability

Adenoma–Carcinoma Sequence:
Normal mucosa
↓ APC mutation
Aberrant crypt foci
↓ KRAS mutation
Adenoma
↓ p53 mutation
Carcinoma

Microsatellite Instability (MSI)

  • Mismatch repair deficiency
  • Lynch syndrome (germline)
  • Sporadic (MLH1 hypermethylation)
  • ~15% of colorectal cancers
  • Better prognosis
  • Immunotherapy response

CIMP Pathway

  • CpG island methylation
  • Associated with BRAF mutations
Clinical Implication: MSI-high tumors respond differently to chemo and immunotherapy

Section D

Clinical Presentation

Early Symptoms

Common Presentations

  • Rectal bleeding (most common)
  • Change in bowel habits
  • Tenesmus and urgency
  • Mucus discharge
  • Incomplete evacuation feeling
⚠ Red Flags:
• Bleeding + change in bowel habits
• Age >50 with new symptoms
• Family history + symptoms
• Iron-deficiency anemia
Do NOT attribute rectal bleeding to hemorrhoids without examination

Advanced Symptoms

Local Disease

  • Pelvic/rectal pain
  • Palpable mass on DRE
  • Obstructive symptoms
  • Fistula or incontinence

Systemic Disease

  • Constitutional: weight loss, fatigue
  • Anemia: iron deficiency
  • Metastatic: hepatomegaly, dyspnea, bone pain

Complications

Obstruction

  • Cramping pain
  • Distension
  • Constipation
  • Vomiting (late)

Perforation

  • Peritonitis
  • Sepsis
  • Emergency surgery

Invasion

  • Fistula (bladder, vagina)
  • Pelvic organs
  • Distant metastases
Emergency presentations require urgent stabilization before definitive staging

Section E

Diagnostic Workup

Initial Clinical Assessment

History

  • Symptom timeline
  • Risk factor screening
  • Family history
  • Prior colonoscopies

Physical Exam

  • General: weight, vital signs
  • Abdomen: mass, hepatomegaly
  • Digital rectal exam (DRE)

Baseline Labs

  • CBC: anemia
  • Electrolytes
  • Liver function tests
  • CEA (baseline for surveillance)
  • Coagulation if surgery planned
CEA: prognostic marker, not diagnostic. Useful for monitoring.

Digital Rectal Exam (DRE)

Technique

  1. Patient positioning (left lateral or lithotomy)
  2. Inspect perianal area
  3. Palpate sphincters (tone)
  4. Sweep circumferentially
  5. Assess:
    • Location (clock position)
    • Size
    • Fixation vs mobility
    • Distance from anal verge

Yield

  • Detects low rectal tumors
  • Guides urgency of endoscopy
  • Informs surgical planning (sphincter involvement)
DRE can detect up to 70% of rectal tumors. Never skip it.

Colonoscopy

Gold Standard

  • Full colonoscopy with biopsy
  • Visualize and biopsy tumor
  • Map synchronous lesions:
    • 2–5% synchronous CRC
    • 30% polyps

Tissue Goals

  • Histology: adenocarcinoma, grade
  • Molecular testing:
    • MSI / MMR status
    • KRAS, NRAS (metastatic disease)
    • BRAF (prognosis)
Tattoo location if tumor difficult to localize on imaging

Pelvic MRI (High-Resolution)

MRI is the CORNERSTONE of local staging and surgical planning

Indication

  • Staging of all rectal cancers
  • T, N, CRM, EMVI assessment

Key Sequences

  • High-resolution T2-weighted
  • Multiplanar imaging
  • Axial, coronal, sagittal oblique

Essential Report Elements

  • T stage
  • Nodal status (N stage)
  • Distance to mesorectal fascia (CRM)
    • ≤1 mm = threatened/positive
  • EMVI (extramural vascular invasion)
  • Relationship to:
    • Sphincters
    • Levators
    • Adjacent organs
  • Lateral pelvic nodes

CT Thorax-Abdomen-Pelvis (TAP)

Indication

  • Systemic staging
  • Detect metastases:
    • Liver
    • Lung
    • Peritoneum
    • Other organs

Additional Imaging

  • PET-CT: selective use
    • Indeterminate liver lesions
    • Recurrence assessment
    • Not routine staging
Contrast-enhanced CT chest or dedicated chest CT for lung metastasis detection

Endorectal Ultrasound (ERUS)

Best Use

  • Early tumors: T1–T2
  • High accuracy for T staging (operator-dependent)
  • Real-time assessment
  • Guides local excision decisions

Limitations

  • Advanced/stenosing lesions
  • Upper rectal tumors
  • Less reliable for nodal assessment
  • Operator-dependent
ERUS complements MRI for early disease; MRI superior for advanced tumors

Section F

Staging & Extension Assessment

Putting Imaging into Staging

MRI + CT TAP + Colonoscopy = Complete Staging

MRI: Local Staging

  • T stage accuracy ~85–90%
  • N stage accuracy ~70–75%
  • CRM: most critical
    • ≤1 mm = positive
    • Major predictor of local recurrence
  • EMVI: higher systemic spread risk

Management-Changing Factors

  • CRM ≤1 mm → neoadjuvant therapy
  • EMVI positive → aggressive approach
  • T3c/d or T4 → neoadjuvant therapy
  • N+ → neoadjuvant therapy
  • Lateral nodes >7mm → consider LPND
  • Low tumor close to sphincter → neoadjuvant to preserve

Staging-to-Management Mapping

Stage TNM Primary Approach
I T1–T2, N0, M0 Consider direct surgery (TME or local excision if T1 favorable)
II T3–T4, N0, M0 Neoadjuvant therapy if threatened CRM/EMVI, then TME
III Any T, N+, M0 Neoadjuvant CRT/TNT → TME → adjuvant chemo
IV Any T, Any N, M1 Systemic therapy ± metastasis-directed therapy ± primary resection
All cases should be discussed in MDT for individualized planning

Section G

Therapeutic Management

Multidisciplinary Tumor Board (MDT)

MDT discussion is MANDATORY for all rectal cancer cases

Core Team

  • Colorectal surgeon
  • Medical oncologist
  • Radiation oncologist
  • Radiologist
  • Pathologist
  • Gastroenterologist
  • Specialist nurse

MDT Role

  • Review staging imaging
  • Individualize treatment plan
  • Decide on:
    • Neoadjuvant therapy (type, duration)
    • Surgical approach
    • Adjuvant therapy
    • Surveillance protocol
  • Discuss organ preservation options

Radiotherapy

Indications

  • T3–T4 tumors
  • Node-positive disease
  • Close/threatened CRM
  • EMVI positive
  • Sphincter preservation attempt

Techniques

  • Short-course RT:
    • 5 × 5 Gy (25 Gy total)
    • Surgery within 1 week OR delay 6–12 weeks (TNT)
  • Long-course CRT:
    • 45–50.4 Gy in 25–28 fractions
    • Concurrent fluoropyrimidine
    • Surgery 6–12 weeks later
  • IMRT/VMAT: reduce toxicity
Goals: Downstage tumor • Improve local control • Increase resectability • Sphincter preservation

Chemotherapy Protocols

Concurrent (CRT)

  • 5-FU continuous infusion
  • Capecitabine oral
  • Radiosensitizer role

Neoadjuvant (TNT)

  • FOLFOX or CAPOX
  • Before or after CRT
  • ↑ pCR rates
  • ↑ compliance
  • Early systemic control

Adjuvant

  • Stage II high-risk
  • Stage III
  • FOLFOX or CAPOX
  • 3–6 months

Metastatic Disease

  • FOLFOX, FOLFIRI, FOLFOXIRI
  • + Bevacizumab (anti-VEGF)
  • + Cetuximab/panitumumab (anti-EGFR, if RAS wild-type)

MSI-High Tumors

  • Immunotherapy:
  • Pembrolizumab
  • Nivolumab ± ipilimumab
  • Superior to chemo in MSI-high metastatic disease

Surgery — Principles & Techniques

Total Mesorectal Excision (TME) is the GOLD STANDARD

TME Principles

  • Sharp dissection in embryologic plane
  • Remove mesorectum en bloc
  • Includes:
    • Mesorectal fat
    • Lymph nodes
    • Intact mesorectal fascia
  • Goal: negative CRM

Surgical Options

  • Low Anterior Resection (LAR):
    • Sphincter-saving
    • Coloanal anastomosis if low
    • Consider diverting ileostomy
  • Abdominoperineal Resection (APR):
    • When sphincter cannot be preserved
    • Permanent colostomy

Advanced Surgical Techniques

Local Excision

  • TEM (Transanal Endoscopic Microsurgery)
  • TAMIS (Transanal Minimally Invasive Surgery)
  • Indications:
    • T1 favorable lesions
    • Well/moderate differentiation
    • No LVI
    • Sm1 invasion (<1000 μm)
    • <3 cm size

Transanal TME (TaTME)

  • Bottom-up approach
  • Better distal margin in low tumors
  • Requires expertise
  • Selected centers

Lateral Pelvic Node Dissection

  • Consider if confirmed lateral node mets
  • Regional practice variation
  • Common in Japan

Surgical Margins

Negative CRM is the MOST IMPORTANT predictor of local control

Circumferential Resection Margin (CRM)

  • Positive: ≤1 mm
  • Major predictor of:
    • Local recurrence
    • Distant metastases
    • Overall survival
  • TME quality determines CRM status

Distal Margin

  • Historical: 2 cm rule
  • Modern evidence:
    • ≤1 cm acceptable with good TME
    • Even millimetric margins safe after preop CRT
  • Intramural spread limited after CRT
  • Enables sphincter preservation
TME quality trumps absolute margin measurements. Good TME with 5mm distal margin beats poor TME with 2cm margin.

Organ-Preserving Strategies

"Watch-and-Wait"

  • Indication: complete clinical response (cCR) after neoadjuvant therapy
  • Selection criteria:
    • No palpable tumor (DRE)
    • No visible tumor (endoscopy)
    • No residual tumor (MRI)
  • Surveillance:
    • DRE + endoscopy every 3 months × 2 years
    • MRI every 3–6 months × 2 years

Outcomes & Considerations

  • Success rate: 80–90% organ preservation at 3 years
  • Regrowth: ~25% by 3 years
    • Most salvageable with surgery
  • Requires:
    • Motivated, compliant patient
    • Experienced center
    • Rigorous follow-up
Watch-and-wait is NOT standard of care — research protocol or highly selected patients only

Section H

Follow-up & Surveillance

Surveillance Principles

Goals

  • Detect local recurrence early
  • Detect metachronous metastases amenable to curative treatment
  • Monitor for metachronous polyps/cancers
  • Assess functional outcomes

General Schedule

  • Years 0–2:
    • Clinic + CEA: q3–6 months
    • CT TAP: annually
  • Years 3–5:
    • Clinic + CEA: q6 months
    • CT TAP: annually
  • Colonoscopy:
    • 1 year post-surgery
    • Then at 3 years
    • Then every 5 years if normal
Proctoscopy or pelvic MRI for low rectal cancers with anastomosis or organ preservation

Red Flags for Recurrence

Laboratory

  • Rising CEA
  • New anemia
  • Abnormal LFTs

Clinical

  • New pelvic pain
  • Rectal bleeding
  • Change in bowel habits
  • Palpable mass
  • Weight loss

Systemic

  • Respiratory symptoms
  • Abdominal pain
  • Hepatomegaly
  • Bone pain
Most recurrences occur within first 2 years — intensive surveillance during this period

Section I

Prognosis

Stage-Based Outcomes

Approximate 5-Year Overall Survival

Stage 5-Year OS Key Prognostic Factors
I 85–95% T1 vs T2, grade
II 60–80% T3 depth, EMVI, CRM, grade, MSI
III 40–70% Nodal burden (N1 vs N2), T stage, CRM, EMVI
IV 10–20% Resectability of mets, molecular profile, response to therapy

Favorable Prognostic Factors

  • Early T/N stage
  • Negative CRM
  • No EMVI
  • Well/moderate differentiation
  • MSI-high
  • pCR after neoadjuvant therapy

Adverse Prognostic Factors

  • Positive CRM
  • EMVI positive
  • Poor differentiation
  • High nodal burden (N2)
  • Mucinous/signet ring histology
  • BRAF mutation (sporadic MSS)

Section J

Key Take-Home Messages

Key Take-Home Messages

1. Rectal cancer management requires MDT planning
2. MRI is cornerstone for local staging and operative planning
3. TME with negative CRM is essential for local control
4. Neoadjuvant therapy is standard in T3–T4 or N+ disease
5. Consider organ preservation only in strict protocols with rigorous follow-up
6. Molecular profiling matters for metastatic disease and immunotherapy
7. Surveillance with CEA, imaging, and colonoscopy detects treatable recurrences

Section L

Practical Pearls for OR & Clinic

Clinical & Surgical Pearls

Preoperative

• MRI quality matters — demand dedicated rectal protocol with CRM reporting
• Tattoo tumor location endoscopically if planning limited resection
• Pre-op stoma counseling — involve stoma nurse early if APR likely
• Optimize nutrition — consider supplementation if malnourished

Intraoperative

• Aim for negative CRM — avoid piecemeal excision
• Diverting ileostomy for low coloanal anastomosis (reduces clinical leak)
• Intact TME specimen — pathologist should assess quality

Postoperative

• Communicate function outcomes: LAR syndrome, sexual/urinary dysfunction risk
• Review pathology at MDT — confirm ypTNM, CRM, adjuvant plan

Long-term

• Screen for LAR syndrome — biofeedback and dietary modifications help
• Address QOL issues — sexual function, body image, psychosocial support

Appendix A

Rapid Clinical Checklist

Checklist for Rounding

□ DIAGNOSIS
  ├─ Biopsy-confirmed adenocarcinoma
  ├─ Histologic grade documented
  └─ MSI/MMR status obtained (especially if <50 yo or Lynch concern)

□ STAGING IMAGING COMPLETE
  ├─ Pelvic MRI with rectal protocol
  │   ├─ T stage
  │   ├─ N stage
  │   ├─ CRM distance (≤1 mm = positive)
  │   ├─ EMVI status
  │   └─ Relationship to sphincters/organs
  ├─ CT TAP (systemic staging)
  └─ Colonoscopy (synchronous lesions)

□ MDT DISCUSSION
  ├─ Case presented with imaging
  ├─ Treatment plan decided:
  │   ├─ Neoadjuvant therapy (type, duration)
  │   ├─ Surgical approach (LAR vs APR, TaTME consideration)
  │   ├─ Adjuvant therapy plan
  │   └─ Organ preservation consideration
  └─ Patient counseled on plan

□ PRE-OP OPTIMIZATION (if surgery planned)
  ├─ Nutrition assessment/supplementation
  ├─ Smoking cessation
  ├─ Stoma counseling (if APR or high-risk anastomosis)
  ├─ Anesthesia clearance
  └─ VTE prophylaxis plan

□ POST-OP PATHOLOGY REVIEW
  ├─ ypTNM documented
  ├─ CRM status (positive ≤1 mm)
  ├─ Tumor regression grade (TRG) if neoadjuvant Rx
  ├─ TME quality assessment
  ├─ Lymph node yield adequate (≥12 nodes)
  └─ Review at MDT → adjuvant therapy decision

□ ADJUVANT THERAPY PLAN
  ├─ Indications: Stage III, high-risk Stage II
  ├─ FOLFOX or CAPOX × 3–6 months
  └─ Coordination with medical oncology

□ SURVEILLANCE SCHEDULE
  ├─ Clinic + CEA q3–6 months × 2 years
  ├─ CT TAP annually × 3–5 years
  ├─ Colonoscopy at 1, 3 years, then q5 years
  └─ Proctoscopy/MRI for LAR or W&W patients
